# Hindu Mythology as an AI Film Genre

Hindu mythology — stories from the Ramayana, the Mahabharat, and associated deity cycles (Hanuman, Krishna, Durga, Kali) — has emerged as the first genre where Indian studios are betting on **fully AI-generated** content, rather than AI-assisted or AI-dubbed content. It is the tip of the spear for [[concepts/ai-filmmaking-india]] (Source: [[sources/2026-04-04-reuters-ai-indian-films]]).

## Why mythology is a natural fit for AI-generated content

- **Durable, pre-sold demand.** Hindu mythology is a popular and durable genre in India with millions of devout followers. An earlier (non-AI) TV adaptation of the Mahabharat drew 200 million viewers between 1988 and 1990 — the audience exists regardless of production method.
- **Stylization masks AI limitations.** Mythological content is expected to look otherworldly: flying gods, divine beings, stylized cosmologies. Viewers tolerate (and often expect) visual conventions that would look fake in a contemporary drama. This hides the remaining weaknesses of generative video.
- **Production economics are brutal at traditional scale.** The Ramayana scene of Hanuman flying while carrying a mountain, or the Mahabharat battle sequences, are exactly the kinds of VFX-heavy shots where AI's cost advantage is largest.
- **No rights entanglements.** Public-domain mythological characters avoid the consent and rights issues that would accompany AI renderings of living actors or licensed IP.

## The active slate

- **[[entities/collective-artists-network]] / Galleri5** is planning eight AI-generated titles focused on Hindu deities: Hanuman, Krishna, Durga, Kali.
- Its first episodic series — an AI Mahabharat — airs on **[[entities/jiostar]]** and has recorded **at least 26.5 million views** since its October release.

## The reception paradox

Commercial and critical signals point opposite directions. The Galleri5/JioStar Mahabharat holds a **1.4/10 rating on IMDb**, with reviewers citing lip-sync issues and "unnatural styling" — yet it clears tens of millions of views. JioStar executive Alok Jain framed it as "a mix of appreciation and healthy debate." The working hypothesis among studios is that durable mythology demand overrides quality complaints, at least in this genre.

Director Anurag Kashyap summarized the studio incentive to Reuters: "In India, cinema isn't about art. It's purely business, so studios are going to use it to make mythologicals. Our audience is a sucker for it."

## Open questions

- Does mythology audience tolerance transfer to other stylized genres (fantasy, sci-fi, superhero), or is it uniquely pre-sold?
- Will religious/devotional audiences ever push back on AI depictions of deities on theological grounds?
- As AI video quality improves, will the genre shift from "AI is acceptable because visuals are stylized" to "AI is acceptable everywhere"?

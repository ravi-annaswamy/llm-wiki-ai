# Coral Hart

Pseudonymous romance novelist profiled by Alexandra Alter in The New York Times in February 2026, and cited in the New Yorker essay on AI and writing as the clearest near-term model of industrial-scale AI fiction (Source: [2026-04-04-newyorker-is-it-wrong-to-write-with-ai](../sources/2026-04-04-newyorker-is-it-wrong-to-write-with-ai.md)).

## The workflow

Hart has built what the New Yorker essay calls an AI-assisted "novel factory" ([ai-novel-factory](../concepts/ai-novel-factory.md)):

- She prompts her system into motion with a scenario (example cited: "a rancher who falls for a city girl running away from her past").
- The system produces a draft ready for human revision in **~45 minutes**.
- Hart revises, then publishes on Amazon under **dozens of different pen names**.
- She has produced **hundreds** of novels this way.
- None have become best-sellers.
- She reportedly makes **six figures** from the method.
- She also sells online classes to aspiring AI-assisted romance novelists — productizing the workflow itself as a secondary revenue stream.

## Disclosure

Amazon asks Hart to disclose AI use. Two secondary sources citing Alexandra Alter's NYT reporting characterize her compliance differently:

- **The New Yorker (2026-04-04):** she "sometimes does and sometimes doesn't."
- **The Verge (2026-04-04, per Jess Weatherbed):** "She doesn't have a label on any of her books that discloses they were written using AI tools," citing her own fear that disclosure would "damage her business for that work" because of the "strong stigma" around the technology.

> ⚠️ CROSS-SOURCE DISCREPANCY: Both secondary sources attribute the claim to the same NYT reporting. The wiki cannot resolve which characterization is correct without the original Alter piece in `raw/`. The Verge's version is the stronger claim (zero disclosure, with a motivation attached); the New Yorker's version is weaker (partial compliance, no stated motivation). Conservatively, the Verge's stronger-stated version is less likely to arise from paraphrase error, but this is speculative.

Either way, the wiki's reading of Hart as a **disclosure-regime stress test** holds: her non-compliance (whether total or partial) is driven by a rational economic calculation — she believes the label itself would depress sales, which the [shy-girl-ai-novel](shy-girl-ai-novel.md) cancellation suggests is probably correct. This makes her the canonical example of how [ai-in-creative-writing](../concepts/ai-in-creative-writing.md) erodes the "implicit understandings" readers rely on, and also of why [human-made-content-labelling](../concepts/human-made-content-labelling.md) faces an uphill fight against the producer side of the market: the people who would most benefit from staying unlabelled have a concrete revenue reason not to cooperate with any scheme.

## Why it matters

Hart is what the future implied by the Shy Girl incident ([shy-girl-ai-novel](shy-girl-ai-novel.md)) looks like when done **deliberately**. The New Yorker essay reads her as:

1. **Authors as showrunners.** Hart is not writing prose word-by-word; she is supervising an AI writers' room, picking premises, editing drafts, and managing a portfolio of pen names. This slots directly into the authors-as-showrunners framing the essay develops.
2. **A commercial middle ground.** Hart's books aren't best-sellers, but they are a six-figure small business. The interesting economic zone for AI fiction may not be the literary top or the viral bottom — it may be the mid-market at volumes individual human authors cannot sustain.
3. **A disclosure-regime stress test.** Amazon's voluntary disclosure request with inconsistent compliance is probably the weakest possible enforcement regime. If publishing develops stronger disclosure norms, Hart's workflow is the natural early casualty or the natural early beneficiary, depending on how readers react.

# Coral Hart

> **Pseudonymous romance novelist running a six-figure AI novel factory.** Hundreds of books across dozens of pen names, ~45 minutes per draft, inconsistent disclosure. The clearest working example of industrial AI fiction as a small business.

Profiled by Alexandra Alter in The NYT (Feb 2026); cited as the canonical case in both the New Yorker essay on AI and writing and The Verge's survey of human-made labelling.

## The workflow

| Step | Detail |
|---|---|
| Input | Scenario prompt (e.g., "a rancher who falls for a city girl running away from her past") |
| Draft | ~45 minutes to a human-revisable draft |
| Revision | Hart edits |
| Publish | Amazon, under dozens of pen names |
| Volume | Hundreds of novels |
| Revenue | Six figures |
| Secondary business | Online classes teaching the workflow |

No title has become a best-seller. The business is the **portfolio**, not the hit.

## Disclosure — and a cross-source discrepancy

Amazon asks Hart to disclose AI use. Two secondary sources, both citing Alter's NYT reporting, characterize her compliance differently:

- **New Yorker (2026-04-04):** she "sometimes does and sometimes doesn't."
- **Verge (2026-04-04):** "doesn't have a label on any of her books," citing her own fear that disclosure would "damage her business for that work."

> ⚠️ **CROSS-SOURCE DISCREPANCY.** Same primary source (Alter/NYT), two different renderings. The Verge's version is stronger (zero disclosure, with motivation); the New Yorker's is weaker (partial compliance). Conservatively preferring the Verge version — harder to reach by paraphrase error — but unresolved without the original NYT piece in `raw/`.

Either way, the wiki reading holds: Hart's non-compliance is a rational economic calculation. The [shy-girl-ai-novel](shy-girl-ai-novel.md) cancellation is evidence she's right to be cautious.

## Why she matters

1. **Authors as showrunners.** Hart supervises an AI writers' room; she does not write word-by-word. The authorship-as-layered argument on [layered-authorship](../concepts/layered-authorship.md) treats this as the fiction analogue of television showrunning.
2. **The commercial mid-market, not the literary top.** Hart's books don't win prizes and don't chart. They occupy a zone previously impossible for one person: mid-market genre fiction at portfolio scale. See [ai-novel-factory](../concepts/ai-novel-factory.md).
3. **A disclosure-regime stress test.** She is the producer-side case study for why [human-made-content-labelling](../concepts/human-made-content-labelling.md) faces an uphill fight: the operators who would most benefit from staying unlabelled have concrete revenue reasons not to cooperate.
4. **An own-your-substrate instance.** Hart owns the compounding layers — pen-name portfolio, prompt library, editing loop, course business — and rents the commodity layer (LLM API). See [own-your-substrate](../analyses/own-your-substrate.md).

# Pangram

> **AI-detection firm whose 78%-AI finding on [shy-girl-ai-novel](shy-girl-ai-novel.md) led Hachette to cancel the book.** The only AI-detection vendor named in the wiki's corpus, and the reference point for thinking about the enforcement side of any [ai-in-creative-writing](../concepts/ai-in-creative-writing.md) disclosure regime.

## Why it matters

If publishers, platforms, or prize juries want to know whether a manuscript is AI-generated, they need a firm like Pangram to produce a number. The Shy Girl case shows a Big Five publisher treating a detection score as credible enough to act on.

But the April 2026 Verge piece on [human-made-content-labelling](../concepts/human-made-content-labelling.md) takes an implicitly dim view of the whole AI-detection category, calling detection services *"notoriously unreliable"* in the context of labelling schemes (e.g. *No-AI-Icon*) that depend on them. The structural argument: **any human-made label whose verification routes through an AI detector inherits that detector's reliability ceiling and degrades as models improve at evasion.** This favors process-based verification (manual audit of drafts) or identity-based verification ([proof-i-did-it](proof-i-did-it.md)) over output-based detection.

## Open questions the Shy Girl case raises

- **What does "78% AI-generated" actually mean?** Per-sentence classification? Stylometric similarity? The source does not say.
- **What are Pangram's false-positive / false-negative rates on book-length text?**
- **How does detection interact with appeals** — e.g. Ballard's claim that a freelance editor ran the manuscript through AI without her consent?

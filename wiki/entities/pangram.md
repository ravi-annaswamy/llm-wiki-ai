# Pangram

AI-detection firm that analyzed the horror novel [shy-girl-ai-novel](shy-girl-ai-novel.md) and declared it **78% AI-generated** — a finding that directly led to Hachette's cancellation of the book's publication (Source: [2026-04-04-newyorker-is-it-wrong-to-write-with-ai](../sources/2026-04-04-newyorker-is-it-wrong-to-write-with-ai.md)).

## Relevance to this wiki

Pangram is the only AI-detection vendor named in the current corpus, and its role in the Shy Girl incident makes it the reference point for thinking about the enforcement side of [ai-in-creative-writing](../concepts/ai-in-creative-writing.md) disclosure regimes. If publishers, platforms, or prize juries want to know whether a manuscript is AI-generated, they need a firm like Pangram to produce a number. The 78% figure was treated by the publisher (Hachette) as credible enough to act on.

## Open questions the Shy Girl case raises

- **What does "78% AI-generated" actually mean?** The New Yorker essay does not explain Pangram's methodology. Is it a per-sentence classification averaged across the text? A stylometric similarity score against known model outputs? An entirely different approach?
- **What are the false-positive and false-negative rates?** Detection of AI text is a known-hard problem, and publicly available detectors have had mixed track records.
- **What happens when models get better at evading detection?** The Shy Girl incident is a data point from a specific moment in the adversarial cycle. The relevant question for publishers is whether detection stays viable long-term.
- **What's the norm for author appeals?** Mia Ballard publicly suggested a freelance editor ran the manuscript through AI without her consent. How does a detection firm's finding interact with that kind of dispute?

None of these are answered by the source, but they are the obvious next questions for any reader trying to understand whether AI-detection is a viable part of a disclosure regime in publishing.
